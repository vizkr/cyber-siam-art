## The Cosmology: The Three-Body System

The cosmology of Cyber Siam operates as a three-body system:

1. **The Digital Gods in Ancient Temples:** The AI, the oracle, the code finding its home in the sacred geometry of the past.
2. **The Ancient Gods in Digital Temples:** The spirits, the monks, the old prayers moving through the circuit boards and fibre optics of the present.
3. **The Meat Puppet:** The human, the viewer, the host standing between the two temples, neither of which is quite real.

The camera inhabits the POV of the meat puppet. We are looking through a body standing at the intersection.

The circuit board is not a metaphor for the temple. The circuit board is the latest temple the ancients haven't built yet. The geometry is the same because the function is the same: moving something sacred from one point to another without loss.

## The Visual Language

### The Perfect Loop

The visual structure is not a linear journey; it is a perfect loop. The same but different. 

A monk enters a corridor → becomes data → data becomes the temple → the temple becomes the returning monk. Frame one is frame last. The playback mechanic itself is the cosmology. The Ken Burns drift is replaced or augmented by structural loops and slow rotation, reinforcing the mandala nature of the Khmer temple layouts.

### The Convergence Zone

The visual space is the transition — the moment where stone and circuit trace dissolve into each other. Neither is the original. Both are.

The palette must be anchored in real temple materials, not default cyberpunk neon.
- **Base:** Laterite (red-brown pored stone), sandstone, soot, monsoon-stained prang green-black.
- **Punctuation:** One electric tone — cyan or gold — used rarely. A single trace flaring once, not a base coat.
- **The Monks:** Encountered, never explained. Semi-transparent, existing in both states simultaneously.

### The Music Leads

The music is not the soundtrack; it *is* the temple the visuals are standing in. 

The unique signature of Cyber Siam is classical Thai instrumentation (pin, khaen, ranat, sor duang) fed through dark electronic processing and distortion chains. The music must be composed first or in parallel, and the images cut to the phrasing of the Thai instruments.
