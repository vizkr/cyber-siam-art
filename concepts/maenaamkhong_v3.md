## Language notes

**The principle:** each line is in the language that makes it most true. Not a policy — a decision made line by line.

**Thai** carries the channel's signature words — สายไฟ, สายน้ำ, the river's name. These do not survive translation.

**English** delivers the hard, universal images — "The lines on the map can be erased", "Soldiers stand on opposite banks", "They came with maps and flags." Facts. Plain speech.

**The monks-chanting line (Verse 2)** now performs what it asserts. Three languages of Buddhist chant appear in the line itself — Thai, Lao (ສວດມົນ — *suad mon*, chanting), Khmer (ស្ម័គ្រចិត្ត — *smak chet*, devotion/willing heart) — before resolving on "but the same Buddha." The line demonstrates multilingual sacred life rather than just describing it.

**The outro is the river speaking in its own languages, in the order the water runs:**
- Lao: ເຮົາຄືກັນ (*Hao khue kan* — We are the same) — Yunnan/Isan, the upper river
- Thai: เราคือคนเดียวกัน — the mid-river
- Khmer: យើងដូចគ្នា (*Yeung doch knea* — We are the same) — Angkor, the great lake
- Vietnamese: Chúng ta là một (*Chung ta la mot* — We are one) — the delta, the sea

The Vietnamese line arrives last because it is the last language the river speaks before it reaches the sea. The original beautiful move is preserved — one word from the far end, arriving last — but now it is the culmination of a real sequence, not a token.

Short, colloquial forms used throughout to avoid political connotations of "nation" or "ethnic group."

## Style Guide — Cyber Nagara

**Paste into Suno style field:**
`dark cinematic electronic, Thai traditional instruments processed through dark ambient, deep sub bass, slow build, cinematic drops, multilingual Thai English Lao Khmer Vietnamese, ancient future, atmospheric, 110 BPM`

**Production notes:**
- Intro: sparse — single processed khim, ambient river texture, spoken delivery
- Verse 1: khim melody, temple bells, sub bass low and distant. Thai and English alternate naturally
- Chorus: full hit — khim + ranat + sub bass. The emotional peak. Thai lines carry the weight
- Verse 2: saw duang enters, darker register. The monks-chanting line should carry audible tonal shifts between the three languages — Thai, Lao, Khmer — before the resolution line
- Bridge: energy drops. Almost spoken. Then rebuilds — defiance, unshakeable. ส่งต่อ is the key verb — transmission, forwarding, the river as data bus
- Final Verse: vast and open. Ranat drops out. Khim alone over bass
- Outro: the river speaks. Each language enters as the water moves downstream. Lao first, Vietnamese last. Fade into river current sound — the signal dissolving back into the water
